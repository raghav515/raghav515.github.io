There was no combat robotics team at NITK when I started. No seniors to ask, no spare parts bin, no blueprint. Just a conviction that we could build something that hit hard and kept working.

## The pitch came before the robot

Before any metal was cut, I had to convince people this was worth funding. I wrote a management proposal and pitched it — and walked out with over **$3,000**. That number mattered less as money than as permission: someone believed the idea enough to back it.

## Building GNARLY

We chose the hard path — a **four-wheel-drive** design when most 15 kg bots run two — because it gave us control when the arena got chaotic. We failed a lot. Weapons that stripped their belts. Drivetrains that shook loose mid-match. Each failure taught the team something no lecture had.

## What it became

By the end, GNARLY wasn't just a robot. It was a program — one that outlived my time driving it, with a team that could design, fund, and fight on its own. That's the part I'm proudest of: not what I built, but what could keep building after me.
